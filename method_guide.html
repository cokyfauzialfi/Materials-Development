Research Reporting

Based on APA 7th Edition Standards (2019b)

The Anatomy of an SLA Report

A typical research paper follows a systematic structure to ensure clarity and logical flow.

  • 1
    Abstract: A brief summary of the entire study (approx. 150-250 words).
  • 2
    Introduction: Background, literature review, and the gap the study fills.
  • 3
    Methods: Details on participants, materials, and procedures.
  • 4
    Results: Statistical or descriptive analysis of findings.
  • 5
    Discussion: Interpreting results in light of theory and previous research.

Generating Questions

Turning an interest into a testable hypothesis.

Finding the "Gap"

Effective research questions usually arise from a careful review of existing literature. Focus on:

Information Gaps

Where existing studies have conflicting results or haven't explored a specific variable.

Local Context

Applying "grand theories" to your specific classroom or local learners.

Checklist for Good Questions:

  • Are the variables clearly defined and measurable?
  • Is the question specific rather than overly broad?
  • Can it be answered with the resources currently available?
  • Does it have pedagogical or theoretical value?

Feasibility Check

Ensuring the project is achievable.

Time Constraints

Consider your teaching load. Action Research should be "unobtrusive" to the learning process while still being systematic.

Participant Access

Do you have permission to collect data? Are there enough participants to make your findings meaningful?

The Golden Rule of Feasibility

"Narrow the theme to something that can both be studied and is worth studying." (019_split.txt)

Replication & Ethics

Confirming findings and protecting participants.

The Power of Replication

Replication is the process of confirming findings by repeating a study. It ensures that results aren't just one-time flukes.

The IRIS Database

IRIS is a repository of materials used in published SLA research. Using materials from IRIS makes replication faster and more accurate (Marsden et al., 2016).

Ethical Considerations

  • Informed Consent: Participants must know what they are involved in.
  • Confidentiality: Data must be anonymized to protect identities.
